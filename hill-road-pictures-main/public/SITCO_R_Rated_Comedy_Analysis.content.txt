R-Rated Ensemble Comedy
Box Office Trend Analysis — 1978 to 2024
Prepared for: So, I'm the Crazy One? / Hill Road Pictures • March 2026
Executive Summary
R-rated ensemble comedies had a genuine golden age at the domestic box office from roughly 2005 to 2014, routinely
delivering 4–8x returns on modest budgets. Since 2015, studio output in the genre collapsed by over 60% — not
because audiences stopped wanting these films, but because the traditional studio greenlight model stopped working
for mid-budget comedies. The audience gap created by studio withdrawal is real, measurable, and growing. Films built
around built-in audiences with lean budgets consistently outperform. That is the exact market condition So, I'm the
Crazy One? is engineered to exploit.

Three Patterns Every Investor Should Know
0
1
The golden age was real.
Eight films cleared $150M domestic between 2005 and 2014 — Wedding Crashers, The Hangover, Bridesmaids, Ted, 22
Jump Street, and others — most produced on budgets under $50M. ROI multiples of 4–8x were not exceptional; they
were the norm for well-executed ensemble comedies with a hook.
0
2
The collapse was supply-driven, not demand-driven.
Studios cut major R-rated comedy releases by 60%+ starting around 2015 as the streaming wars shifted capital toward
tentpole IP and franchises. Audiences did not stop wanting these films. The studios stopped making them. Demand data
from streaming platforms confirms the genre's appetite remains strong.
0
3
Lean ensemble budgets consistently outperform.
The pattern is clear: films with built-in audience hooks, budgets under $25M, and ensemble casts of recognizable talent
routinely generate the highest ROI multiples in the genre. Neighbors ($18M → $150M, 8.3x), Girls Trip ($19M → $115M,
6.1x), Superbad ($20M → $121M, 6.1x). That is the lane SITCO is designed to occupy.
